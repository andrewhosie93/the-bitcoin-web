# Bubble Charter

This charter governs how bubbles are identified, connected, populated, sourced and reviewed. The project is a contextual graph, not a strict tree. Growth must be deliberate, source-aware and editorially reviewed.

## A. What A Bubble Represents

A bubble represents one canonical concept deserving an independent explanation.

A bubble should normally be:
- distinct
- independently explainable
- necessary to the route
- sourceable
- navigationally useful
- historically or conceptually durable
- potentially reusable across routes

A bubble is not automatically:
- every person
- every date
- every historical event
- every example
- every causal relationship
- every term mentioned in Deeper
- every Bitcoin-adjacent idea

Weaker candidates may belong as:
- an example inside Deeper
- an edge rationale
- a source
- an "Also touches" relationship
- part of another broader node

## B. Bubble Qualification Tests

Use these tests before proposing or approving a bubble:

1. Distinct test
   Is it meaningfully different from an existing node?
2. Explanation test
   Can it be explained independently in one Surface paragraph?
3. Necessity test
   Would removing it create a real gap in the route?
4. Sourceability test
   Are credible sources available?
5. Navigation test
   Does following it deepen the current explanation?
6. Durability test
   Is it more than a temporary example or anecdote?
7. Reusability test
   Could it legitimately connect to more than one route?

## C. Bubble Decision Score

Score each candidate from 0-2 for:

- distinct concept
- necessary to explanation
- sourceable
- useful navigational step
- shared/reusable potential
- durable significance

Guideline:

- 9-12: Strong bubble candidate.
- 6-8: Requires editorial review.
- 0-5: Probably belongs inside another node.

The score informs judgement but does not replace it. A high score can still fail because of duplication, poor sourcing or weak route fit. A lower score may still matter when a human editor has a clear reason for inclusion.

## D. Bubble Categories

Use these categories when planning nodes:

- Concept: Scarcity, Trust, Legal Tender.
- Mechanism: Proof of Work, Difficulty Adjustment, Central Bank Settlement.
- Institution: Central Banks, Banks, Mints.
- Technology: Hash Functions, Digital Signatures, Databases.
- System: Banking Rails, Payment Networks, Fiat Money.
- Practice: Self-Custody, Taxation, Assay.
- Historical hinge: Gold Convertibility, Bretton Woods, Financial Panics.
- Artefact: Banknote, Coin, Ledger, Tally Stick.

People normally belong in Deeper or Sources rather than as structural bubbles. Events become bubbles only when they are genuine causal or historical hinges. Very broad academic fields should be split into useful explanatory concepts.

## E. One Canonical Node, Multiple Contexts

Canonical content exists once. A node may have several parents, may appear at several Path depths, and may be reached through several legitimate routes.

Its visible placement depends on the route travelled. Its content, source records, ID and status remain canonical. There is no mandatory "true host branch" for a shared node.

The graph should prefer one strong shared node over several near-duplicates. Legal Tender may belong to Political Control, Trust and Government & Power contexts without needing duplicate versions. Central Banks may sit under both Fiat Money and Political Control because the explanatory route changes while the canonical concept stays the same.

## F. Edge Doctrine

Every navigable strand must have an explicit meaning. Vague association is not enough.

Navigable edge types:

- Foundation: The child concept underlies the parent.
- Mechanism: The child explains how the parent works.
- Predecessor: The child is an earlier form or historical precursor.
- Implementation: The child puts the parent idea into practice.
- Institution: The child institution administers or shapes the parent system.
- Example: The child is an important concrete instance worthy of independent treatment.

Non-navigable relationship types:

- Related
- Also touches
- Contrast
- Analogy
- Debate

These remain preview-only. Every proposed navigable edge must have a one-sentence rationale answering: "Why does following this line deepen the current explanation?"

## G. Path Depth And Coverage Depth

### Path Depth

Path depth is the number of edges travelled from Bitcoin in the current route.

It is used in the interface. The same canonical node may have different Path depths.

### Coverage Depth

Coverage depth is the shortest approved route from Bitcoin to the canonical node.

It is used for editorial planning and depth-four coverage. Do not store or present coverage depth as though it were the node's only valid position.

## H. Meaning Of "Complete To Depth Four"

Do not define completion as forcing every branch to have children at exactly depth four.

Depth-four coverage means:
- every main strand has a coherent depth-two structure
- depth-two hubs have meaningful depth-three concepts
- each major hub has at least one strong route reaching depth four where appropriate
- every canonical node with coverage depth four or less has Surface, Deeper and Sources
- routes may stop naturally before depth four when further subdivision would create filler

A node is not complete merely because prose exists.

## I. Bubble Size Rules

A bubble is probably too broad when:
- Surface needs several unrelated definitions
- Deeper becomes several separate essays
- it has more than six fundamentally different child directions
- its title represents an entire academic field

A bubble is probably too narrow when:
- Surface and Deeper repeat each other
- it cannot support credible sources
- it is only one example
- removing it would not affect the route
- it exists only to satisfy a depth target

Guidelines:
- title ideally 1-4 words
- short title ideally under 24 characters
- direct children ideally 2-5
- practical maximum of 6 before introducing a hub or split

## J. Public Content Contract

### Summary

- 8-18 words
- one clear sentence
- useful for previews

### Surface

- 15-35 words
- one or two short sentences
- concept first
- no forced Bitcoin justification
- readable in roughly five seconds
- British English

Surface answers:

1. What is this?
2. What is the most important thing to understand?

### Deeper

- normally 180-300 words
- three or four short paragraphs

Suggested structure:

1. concept or historical problem
2. mechanism, trade-off or complexity
3. relevant Bitcoin mechanism or comparison
4. limits of the comparison where useful

Do not force an explicit Bitcoin paragraph where it is unnatural.

### Sources

Normally:
- 2-5 genuinely relevant source records
- primary, technical or institutional material where available
- serious secondary scholarship for historical interpretation
- competing interpretations for contested topics

No invented citations. No source should be attached merely because it mentions the same term. Source cards are not line-level citations.

## K. Source Hierarchy

Preferred order:

1. Primary documents, protocol documentation, legislation and original papers
2. Peer-reviewed research and serious academic books
3. Archives, museums, central banks and institutional publications
4. High-quality technical or historical synthesis
5. Lectures, interviews and podcasts as orientation rather than sole evidence

Bitcoin advocacy material should not be the only basis for contested economic or historical claims.

Institutional sources should not be treated as neutral or complete simply because they are institutional.

## L. Status Lifecycle

Status flow:

Planned -> Draft -> Source-needed -> Human-reviewed -> Sourced

A node may only advance when the relevant requirements are met.

"Sourced" does not mean merely having sourceIds. It means:
- sources have been inspected
- major claims are supported
- contested claims are identified
- human editorial review is complete

## M. Duplicate Prevention

Before creating a node, search for:
- synonyms
- broader existing concepts
- narrower existing concepts
- shared-node opportunities
- overlapping historical roles

Prefer one canonical node with several incoming strands over several near-identical nodes with slightly different wording.

## N. Expansion Workflow

### Stage 1 - Map

Propose:
- candidate nodes
- node types
- parents
- edge types
- edge rationales
- shared nodes
- source categories

No public prose.

### Stage 2 - Human Pruning

Remove:
- duplicates
- filler
- artificial chronology
- politically loaded framing
- weak nodes created only to hit depth four

### Stage 3 - Source Inventory

Identify and inspect suitable sources before writing long explanations.

### Stage 4 - Populate Small Batches

Work in batches of approximately 5-10 nodes:

- Summary
- Surface
- Deeper
- Sources
- Editorial notes
- Validation
- Manual review
- Commit

### Stage 5 - Cross-Strand Review

Identify genuine shared concepts and connect them without duplicating canonical nodes.

## O. Definition Of Done

A bubble is complete only when:

- its inclusion has been approved
- its category is clear
- parent relationships are reviewed
- every navigable edge has a rationale
- duplicate/shared-node review is complete
- Summary is written
- Surface is written
- Deeper is written
- sources are attached and inspected
- British English is checked
- atlas validation passes
- manual editorial review is complete
- no major unresolved sourcing concern remains

## P. Temporal Doctrine

The atlas is a reverse historical atlas, not a conventional timeline.

### 1. Time Is A Backbone, Not The Whole Graph

Time should:
- orient historical movement
- help distinguish predecessors from mechanisms
- encourage every strand to contain genuine historical depth
- reduce arbitrary depth-four filler

Time should not:
- force every conceptual relationship into chronology
- imply one universal linear progression
- assign false precision to disputed origins
- determine every node's parent
- replace causal or explanatory logic

Core rule:

"Time provides the backbone. Causality and explanation provide the branches."

### 2. Distinguish Three Measures

#### Path Depth

Path depth is the number of edges travelled from Bitcoin through the current route.

It is used in the interface. A shared canonical node may have different Path depths.

#### Coverage Depth

Coverage depth is the shortest approved route from Bitcoin to a canonical node.

It is used for editorial planning. Do not treat it as the node's only valid position.

#### Historical Era

Historical era is the period in which a node emerged, operated or became important.

It is used to orient the retrospective history. Historical era must not be treated as another intrinsic graph depth.

### 3. Temporal Kinds

Use temporal kind as planning and review metadata unless the graph schema later explicitly supports it.

```ts
type TemporalKind =
  | "event"
  | "period"
  | "persistent"
  | "recurring"
  | "atemporal"
```

- event: a bounded occurrence or hinge, such as a founding moment, publication, crisis or legal break.
- period: a span in which a practice, system or institution operated or became important.
- persistent: a concept, institution, practice or technology that continues across several eras.
- recurring: a pattern that appears repeatedly in different historical settings.
- atemporal: an abstract mechanism, theorem or conceptual distinction whose explanatory role is not tied to one origin date.

### 4. Temporal Edge Discipline

Predecessor edges normally carry the strongest chronological claim.

Foundation, Mechanism, Implementation, Institution and Example edges may be historical, contemporary or atemporal depending on the explanation. Do not relabel every older thing as a parent merely because it came first.

Where chronology is disputed, approximate or uneven across regions, say so in planning notes and source requirements rather than inventing precision.
