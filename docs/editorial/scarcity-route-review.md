# Scarcity Route Editorial Review

Private editorial notes for the route:

Bitcoin as Money -> Scarcity -> Gold -> Commodity Money -> Costly Production

These notes are not public website content. They identify claims, source coverage, and points that need human review before any node is promoted beyond draft/source-needed.

## Bitcoin as Money

### Main Claims Made

- Bitcoin combines limited issuance, transferable units, public settlement, and direct custody through private keys.
- Bitcoin has predictable issuance rules, divisible units, and bearer-style control through keys.
- Protocol rules can be inspectable without guaranteeing monetary adoption.
- Market valuation, liquidity, legal treatment, custody safety, and user acceptance remain outside the protocol itself.

### Existing Source IDs Attached

- bitcoin-whitepaper
- bitcoin-core-repo
- jevons-money-mechanism
- menger-origin-money
- graeber-debt

### Claims That Still Require Human Verification

- The framing of Bitcoin as combining several monetary properties rather than being reducible to one property.
- The description of Bitcoin divisibility, transferability, and key-based control.
- The distinction between protocol-level issuance rules and market-level valuation.
- Whether the selected monetary theory sources sufficiently support this broad framing.

### Terminology Or Historical Points That May Be Contested

- "Money" can refer to unit of account, medium of exchange, store of value, settlement asset, credit relation, or legal category.
- "Direct custody" may be too simple unless key management, custodial intermediaries, and wallet practices are specified elsewhere.
- "Bearer-style control" should be checked against cases involving multisig, custodians, lost keys, and legal ownership disputes.
- "Monetary network" may need a glossary note if used as a core project term.

### Suggested Additional Source Categories

- Monetary economics overview.
- Monetary anthropology overview.
- Settlement and payment systems history.
- Bitcoin custody and validation references.
- Critical writing on Bitcoin's monetary adoption.

### Editorial Questions For The Human Author

- Should this node define "money" explicitly, or leave definitions distributed across the Money strand?
- Is "market valuation" clear enough for beginners?
- Should Graeber remain attached here, or only on Commodity Money where barter-origin caution is central?

## Scarcity

### Main Claims Made

- Monetary scarcity means credible limits on creating new units, not mere rarity.
- Scarcity can reduce surprise dilution but does not create demand by itself.
- Bitcoin expresses scarcity through consensus rules, validation, incentives, and social coordination.
- Bitcoin scarcity is described as capped issuance, declining issuance, and validation of monetary rules by participating nodes.
- Changes to supply rules are framed as requiring social and network acceptance, not merely a software edit.

### Existing Source IDs Attached

- bitcoin-whitepaper
- bitcoin-core-repo
- jevons-money-mechanism
- menger-origin-money

### Claims That Still Require Human Verification

- The distinction between rarity and credible supply limitation.
- The claim that scarcity alone is insufficient for monetary value.
- The description of Bitcoin issuance as a rule checked by validating users.
- The phrasing around capped issuance, declining issuance, and node validation of block rewards.
- The claim that supply-rule changes require social and network acceptance.

### Terminology Or Historical Points That May Be Contested

- "Credible" may require careful definition: credible to whom, under what enforcement mechanism, and over what time horizon.
- "Consensus rules" may need a separate technical node to avoid overloading this explanation.
- "Capped issuance" should not be made to sound socially automatic or historically uncontested.
- Scarcity in monetary history may involve legal, institutional, customary, and material constraints that do not fit one model.

### Suggested Additional Source Categories

- Monetary theory on scarcity and supply credibility.
- Bitcoin issuance and consensus documentation.
- Critical sources on scarcity-only valuation arguments.
- Historical examples of non-monetary scarce goods.

### Editorial Questions For The Human Author

- Should the node distinguish supply scarcity from liquidity scarcity?
- Should it mention divisibility and recognizability here, or leave those to Gold and Commodity Money?
- Is "social coordination" too abstract for this route stage?

## Gold

### Main Claims Made

- Gold's monetary role came from durability, divisibility, recognizability, costly production, and social acceptance.
- Gold's supply is described as geologically constrained but responsive to prices, technology, discoveries, and extraction costs.
- Gold required supporting systems such as weights, assays, custody, transport, legal standards, and institutions.
- The "digital gold" comparison is useful but limited.

### Existing Source IDs Attached

- bitcoin-whitepaper
- jevons-money-mechanism
- menger-origin-money

### Claims That Still Require Human Verification

- The list of monetary properties attributed to gold.
- The claim that higher prices, new discoveries, better technology, and cheaper extraction can make additional production worthwhile.
- The role of coinage, assay, custody, and legal standards in practical monetary use.
- The appropriateness of using Bitcoin whitepaper support for the gold analogy rather than gold history itself.

### Terminology Or Historical Points That May Be Contested

- "Gold became monetary" risks implying one path or one cause unless handled carefully.
- "Geological scarcity" may need careful qualification because production is constrained but not fixed.
- "Recognizable" may need qualification because verification often required expertise or institutions.
- "Digital gold" may carry market or cultural meanings beyond the narrow supply analogy.

### Suggested Additional Source Categories

- Gold standard history.
- Numismatics and coinage history.
- Assay, minting, and custody references.
- Comparative writing on gold and Bitcoin.

### Editorial Questions For The Human Author

- Should a dedicated gold-history source be added before this node is reviewed?
- Is the current source set too weighted toward monetary theory rather than historical gold practice?
- Should non-monetary uses of gold be expanded or kept brief?

## Commodity Money

### Main Claims Made

- Goods can acquire monetary use when people expect others to accept them.
- Commodity money is not a universal linear origin story from barter.
- Credit, obligation, taxation, ritual exchange, and accounting often coexist with monetary objects.
- Bitcoin resembles commodity money in issuer independence, but differs because it is a digital protocol and ledger.
- Bitcoin is framed as an asset, ledger, rule set, and social coordination problem rather than a traditional physical commodity.

### Existing Source IDs Attached

- jevons-money-mechanism
- menger-origin-money
- humphrey-barter-disintegration
- graeber-debt

### Claims That Still Require Human Verification

- The anthropological caution against a simple barter-origin narrative.
- The description of commodity money as one monetary family rather than the origin of all money.
- The claim that Bitcoin's commodity-money analogy is limited by its lack of physical independent use.
- The claim that Bitcoin's usefulness and scarcity emerge through protocol and network coordination rather than physical consumption.

### Terminology Or Historical Points That May Be Contested

- "Commodity money" has different meanings in economics, legal classification, and historical writing.
- "Accepted later" is close to saleability/liquidity language and may need more precise support.
- "Asset, ledger, rule set, and social coordination problem" may need simplification or glossary support.
- Graeber and monetary economists may frame money's origins differently; the route should not flatten that disagreement.

### Suggested Additional Source Categories

- Monetary anthropology.
- Commodity money case studies.
- Numismatics and standards of weight/quality.
- Legal and economic treatment of Bitcoin as commodity, money, or property.

### Editorial Questions For The Human Author

- Should this node explicitly define barter as a debated explanatory model?
- Should the Bitcoin comparison use "commodity-like" instead of "commodity money"?
- Are Humphrey and Graeber enough to represent the critique, or should additional anthropology sources be added?

## Costly Production

### Main Claims Made

- Costly production can constrain monetary supply expansion.
- Production cost is not the same as value, fairness, or social usefulness.
- Bitcoin uses proof of work so miners spend resources to propose blocks, while nodes verify rules.
- Difficulty adjustment, issuance schedule, and rule-checked block rewards are now part of the explanation.
- Energy expenditure is framed as contributing to block production cost and attack cost, not guaranteeing market value.
- The mining analogy is useful but imperfect.

### Existing Source IDs Attached

- bitcoin-whitepaper
- bitcoin-core-repo
- jevons-money-mechanism

### Claims That Still Require Human Verification

- The comparison between gold production costs and Bitcoin proof-of-work costs.
- The statement that validation is comparatively cheap for users.
- The explanation of difficulty adjustment and its relationship to changing mining power.
- The distinction between block production cost, network security, issuance schedule, and Bitcoin's market value.
- The claim that proof of work raises the cost of attacking transaction history under protocol rules.
- The distinction between mining rewards and the idea of uncovering pre-existing units.

### Terminology Or Historical Points That May Be Contested

- "Costly production" may be confused with labor theory of value or intrinsic value claims.
- "Mining" is metaphorical in Bitcoin and can mislead readers if not carefully framed.
- "Security" language should distinguish attack cost, settlement confidence, and market value.
- Energy use, mining centralization, and security budget debates require specialized sources before deeper expansion.

### Suggested Additional Source Categories

- Proof-of-work literature.
- Bitcoin mining and validation documentation.
- Gold mining economics.
- Security budget and mining centralization debates.
- Energy-use critiques and responses.

### Editorial Questions For The Human Author

- Should proof of work be explained only lightly here and deferred to Energy/Computation?
- Is "comparatively cheap" precise enough for validation without hardware assumptions?
- Should this node mention difficulty adjustment, or would that overload the route?
